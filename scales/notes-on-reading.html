Notes for Reading MOLT Lists

Because many of the scales listed cannot be expressed in the 12 tone scale, the lists numbers the scales from 0 to n-1 for n-equal tempered scales. When it is possible to represent the scales as notes of the 12 tone scale, the lists start with C. When it is possible to translate the scale to the 24 scale this is done, again starting with C.

Currently the algorithm for finding MOLTs is too slow for many scales above the 24 tone scale. The exception to this is scales which are equal to squares of prime numbers. These scales are exceptional because they only have one unique prime factor, which means only one PSS; this limits the number of MOLTs, which lets the algorithm runs fast. Therefore, the list includes prime-squared-equal tempered scales for primes up to 13.

I have not found a good way to find the number of MOLTs for arbitrary tone scales (I'm sure this is trivial for someone who's better at math than I am); however, it seems that for prime squared-tone scales, the number of MOLTs is equal to 2 to the power of its prime factor minus one. For example, the 49 tone scale (72) is equal to 27-1, 64. As a result, I have found that my algorithm runs in a tolerable amount of time for all prime-squared tone scales up to 169 (132).

For n tones, there are 2⌈(n/2)⌉ symmetrical scales. For example, for the 9 tone scale, there are 25 symmetrical scales. Unfortunately, this means finding all the symmetical scale for numbers much larger than 24 becomes increasingly ridiculous (and impossible in a reasonable amount of memory). The 169-tone scale, only having 4096 MOLTs, would have 285 (38,685,626,227,668,133,590,597,632) symmetrical scales. Therefore, for equal tempered scales higher than 25 the corresponding symmetrical scales are not listed.
